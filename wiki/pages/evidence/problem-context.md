# Evidence: Problem Context

## The Problem

Many parents recognise that personal smartphone use encroaches on family time and negatively affects their children's development. Yet **behaviour-change tools framed around reducing screen time fail in practice**:

- Screen-time blockers are circumvented or cause conflict
- Guilt-inducing statistics ("you've been on your phone 5 hours today") produce shame, not change
- Negative reinforcement creates resistance and resentment

## The Approach

The evidence-backed alternative is **positive redirection**: give families something concrete, fun, and structured to do together, rather than telling them to stop doing something. Key elements:

- Structured offline activities reduce opportunity cost of phone use
- Completing activities with children produces intrinsic reward (shared memories, photos)
- Social context (group doing the same challenge) provides external motivation without competitiveness

## Why the Collage Mechanic

The collage is both a progress indicator and a **memory artifact**. This dual function matters:
- Progress indicators (streaks, counters) feel transactional and lose their pull once routine breaks
- Memory artifacts have durable emotional value — a parent is more likely to re-engage if the output has meaning beyond the app

Deliberately avoiding:
- Leaderboards (creates anxiety, not motivation)
- Shame language (defeats the point)
- Notifications as the primary re-engagement mechanism (prototype; deferred to MVP)

## What We Do Not Know

- **Does completing challenges actually change screen-time behaviour?** The app is built on this hypothesis but the prototype does not measure it directly. No research protocol is planned for the prototype cohort.
- **Is group social context motivating or stressful?** The SRS flags this: "light social features are under development and might be dropped, as we want to avoid increasing stress, envy, and fear-of-missing-out." → [questions/open-questions.md](../questions/open-questions.md)
